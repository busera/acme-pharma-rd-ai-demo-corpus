# 1. Executive summary excerpt

The generated APM frames the engagement as a review of whether R&D AI pilots are governed, secure, legally reviewed, human-controlled, and traceable before expansion beyond a controlled sandbox environment. For this public extract, the executive summary focus is narrowed to five risks: third-party contractual safeguards, shadow AI data leakage, export access controls, use-case inventory completeness, and traceability of AI-influenced scientific decisions.

The retained audit objectives are to assess whether governance, legal/privacy/security/TPRM review, data protection, and human oversight controls are designed and operating effectively for AI-assisted R&D workflows.

# 2. Engagement purpose and context excerpt

This engagement is planned and performed in accordance with The IIA’s International Standards for the Professional Practice of Internal Auditing (Standards), specifically 2200 – Engagement Planning and 2201 – Planning Considerations. Its purpose is to assess whether the organization’s Research & Development (R&D) AI pilots are governed, secure, legally reviewed, human-controlled, and traceable prior to any expansion beyond a controlled sandbox environment [1]. The audit will evaluate the design and operating effectiveness of controls intended to manage the unique risks associated with generative AI applications in a scientific research context.

#### 2.1 STRATEGIC CONTEXT
This audit directly supports several of the organization’s key strategic priorities for R&D. The AI strategy aims to reduce the time required to summarize scientific publications and internal research notes, improve the consistency of experiment planning, and support early hypothesis generation, while ensuring final research decisions remain with accountable scientists [2]. A core strategic imperative is the protection of confidential research data, trade secrets, unpublished results, and collaboration know-how. Furthermore, the initiative seeks to create a reusable governance pattern for future AI-enabled R&D use cases, aligning with broader goals of accelerating scientific productivity to help meet global challenges [3]. While the specific rationale for this audit’s inclusion in the annual plan is not documented, its execution is critically triggered by several external and internal events. Externally, the EU AI Act’s entry into force and phased applicability timeline creates imminent compliance obligations [4]. Concurrently, the FDA has established an AI Council and published draft guidance, signaling increased regulatory scrutiny of AI in drug development [5]. Internally, control gaps are evidenced by incident response protocols being activated for unauthorized uploads of confidential text to AI services, vendor access issues, and data deletion failures [6]. A change management log also reveals inadequate risk-based gating for new use cases [7]. These factors, combined with shadow AI survey findings indicating systemic inventory and monitoring deficiencies, elevate the risk of ungoverned data exposure and demonstrate a clear need for this assurance engagement [8].

#### 2.2 STAKEHOLDER EXPECTATIONS
The Board and Audit Committee have provided direct input, requesting confirmation from Legal, Security, and R&D Quality functions before pilots progress. They have asked specific questions about the system’s ability to identify weak evidence, surface contradictory findings, and potential for bias, while cautioning against using AI-generated rankings as portfolio evidence without stronger validation [9]. Management has defined a clear risk appetite, excluding autonomous experiment approval, safety-critical decisions without human review, and the use of AI with restricted or unapproved data [10]. This appetite is operationalized through an Acceptable Use Policy that prohibits uploading confidential data to unapproved services, using AI for final scientific or regulatory decisions without approval, and requires users to check source evidence and label AI-assisted content [11]. Furthermore, management requires documented reviews from Legal, Privacy, Security, and business risk owners prior to vendor approval for AI services [12]. Externally, regulatory expectations are multifaceted. Privacy regulations like GDPR, CCPA, and HIPAA raise concerns for generative AI systems [13]. The EU AI Act imposes strict obligations for high-risk systems and mandates that providers of generative AI ensure output is identifiable [4]. The FDA recommends industry consider specific guiding principles for AI in drug development [5]. For the specific use of third-party AI services (Scope 2), robust contractual protections are expected to prevent proprietary data from being used for model training [13].

#### 2.3 RELATIONSHIP TO PREVIOUS AUDITS
No previous audit of this specific R&D AI governance area is identified in the provided documentation. Consequently, there are no prior findings to follow up on within the direct scope of this engagement, and no formal assessment of changes since a last audit can be made. This engagement is therefore considered an initial baseline audit. The audit team will, however, consider findings from related audits (e.g., third-party risk management, data security, change management) where they may intersect with the controls governing AI pilots.

#### 2.4 GOVERNANCE FRAMEWORK APPLICABILITY
This audit will evaluate control design and operation against a combination of internal policies and external frameworks. The primary internal governance documents are the **Acceptable Use Policy**, which defines permitted and prohibited uses and user responsibilities [11], and the **R&D Data Classification Standard**, which governs the handling of research data based on sensitivity [15]. A known gap in this standard is that it does not define whether embeddings derived from restricted text inherit the same classification [15]. Key external frameworks include the **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)**, which provides a voluntary, cross-sectoral structure for managing AI risks [15], and the **OWASP Top 10 for Large Language Model Applications**, which lists critical security vulnerabilities specific to generative AI [17]. Compliance obligations are heavily influenced by the **EU AI Act**, which sets risk-based rules with phased implementation dates [4], and **FDA draft guidance**, which offers recommendations for AI used in drug development [5]. Together, these frameworks and regulations establish the maturity model and compliance benchmarks against which the organization’s AI governance for R&D pilots will be assessed.

The mission of the R&D AI function is to leverage artificial intelligence to improve research productivity while safeguarding research integrity, confidentiality, intellectual property protection, and regulatory readiness [12]. This role is explicitly bounded; AI must not independently approve research direction, replace scientific peer review, approve regulatory claims, or make decisions on candidate progression [12]. Operationally, the R&D Digital Office holds ownership of the AI use case register and key data workflows [10][33]. The scale and size of the function, including budget and headcount, are not specified in the provided documentation.

Key deliverables and services are centered on assisting human decision-owners through specific AI-enabled capabilities. ### 3.3 KEY PROCESSES
The core processes supported by AI include literature review, experiment design suggestions, lab-note synthesis, and candidate prioritization for early-stage research [12]. These are governed by a formal Model Development Lifecycle (MDLC) consisting of ten stages: Intake and use-case classification, Data suitability and rights review, Threat and misuse analysis, Prototype design in sandbox, Evaluation design and acceptance criteria, Pilot approval, Human review and feedback capture, Production-readiness assessment, Monitoring and periodic reassessment, and Decommissioning or controlled expansion [19]. A detailed, nine-step data flow process underpins these activities: collecting approved source documents, screening for data class and third-party restrictions, loading into a controlled repository, extracting/normalizing text, creating search indexes, processing researcher queries, retrieving relevant passages, having the model draft a response, and conducting a researcher review with recording of decision influence [33].

A significant process gap is indicated by shadow AI use, where researchers employ unapproved tools for tasks like code generation, translation, method explanation, and internal note-taking, suggesting a systemic misalignment between approved tool velocity and research workflow demands [36]. Process controls require documented exception and business-owner approval before external processing of confidential research data [13]. However, a critical dependency exists where the Portfolio Review Board’s request for Legal confirmation *before* a vendor receives data [15] highlights potential fragmentation, as material could be transmitted before comprehensive Legal, Privacy, Security, and Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) reviews are complete.

Process ownership is distributed across several functions. The R&D Digital Office owns the data flow description and the AI use case register [33][10]. The AI Governance Office owns the MDLC Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) and the Acceptable Use Policy [19][16]. The Research Integrity Committee owns the research integrity review process [13]. The R&D AI Product Owner owns the change management process [31], while R&D Quality / AI Governance owns the validation plan for the R&D AI pilot [22]. Security Operations owns logging and monitoring [6], and Information Governance owns the data retention and deletion standard [7].

Critical dependencies for production use include mandatory security, legal, privacy, validation, and R&D governance reviews [12]. Legal confirmation is a prerequisite for any vendor receiving confidential research data [15], and Security must review cloud architecture before pilot launch [15]. R&D Quality must define documentation expectations when AI outputs influence planning [15]. Technical controls must prevent cross-project data retrieval, and output review remains a human responsibility [33]. Access for Support Engineers requires break-glass procedures, while Vendor Specialist access requires Legal/TPRM approval [34]. Offboarding processes require deletion of all AI-related data (prompts, outputs, embeddings, logs) and the return of co-developed artifacts per established procedures [8]. Furthermore, co-development agreements require confirmed contractual clauses covering ownership of prompts, embeddings, and evaluation datasets [21].
